## Overview

This document defines the operational parameters of the RACK Protocol. It presupposes the core semantics defined in [RFC-0001](RFC-0001.md) and may be revised as implementations mature, the ecosystem evolves, or the security landscape changes.

Revisions to this document MUST maintain backward compatibility with RFC-0001 core semantics.

## 1. Payment and Rewards

### 1.1 Design Principles

A Referee can only receive payment via the Keeper. This design aligns with the architectural principle that "the Referee cannot act outside the Keeper" — economic incentives are also placed under Keeper oversight.

By tying payment to **assessment completion** (`ASSESSMENT_ISSUED`) rather than to assessment content, the Referee's compensation is fully independent of the outcome. A Referee cannot increase its payout by issuing favorable assessments.

### 1.2 Payment Flow

**Deposit allocation (STD-029).** The total expected escrow for one assessment equals the Referee's declared `fee.amount`. How that total is split between the parties is the Referee's pricing policy — Claimant-leaning, Actor-leaning, or any ratio — declared in `REFEREE_PROFILE` `fee.deposit` (`claimant` + `actor` MUST equal `fee.amount`). Where no allocation is declared, the requesting party bears the full `fee.amount` and the counterparty's role is `NOT_REQUIRED` in `fee_compliance` — the requester being the Claimant in an initial assessment, or the initiating party of a re-assessment (STD-020); a no-Actor assessment such as PoHI has the Claimant as sole requester. A Referee that wishes to share the cost between parties declares an explicit allocation. The protocol does not prescribe which allocation is appropriate: the allocation is part of the Referee's service design, disclosed in advance so that parties can compare Referees on it.

**Release semantics.** Each Keeper releases only the escrowed balance it holds for the incident. In a split-Keeper topology (Kc and Ka), the amounts released across Keepers sum to the total deposited — the Referee is never paid more than the escrowed total.

**Key prerequisite (split-Keeper).** The pull flow's `FEE_CLAIM` and the `FEE_RECEIPT` that concludes either flow are signed by the Referee, so the paying Keeper must hold the Referee's `public_key` to verify them. A party's Keeper never receives the Referee's sequence-1 `CLAIM_ANCHOR` (the Referee anchors only to its own Keeper), so the Referee supplies the key through its `REFEREE_PROFILE`, published to each party's Keeper before it transacts — in practice together with `INCIDENT_NOTICE (INCIDENT_OPEN)` (RFC-0001 Section 6.19). A Keeper holding no key for the Referee rejects the signed message as undeliverable (`UNKNOWN_TERMINAL`), so an unpublished Referee simply cannot collect — the prerequisite is self-enforcing.

Two payment flows are supported: push-based (Keeper-initiated) and pull-based (Referee-initiated). Both flows conclude with a `FEE_RECEIPT` obligation for the Referee.

**Push-based flow (Keeper-initiated):**
```
1. Actor and Claimant send FEE_DEPOSIT to the Keeper (concurrent with the assessment request)
2. Keeper receives FEE_DEPOSIT and holds funds in escrow
3. After issuing CONTRIBUTION_RESULT, Referee sends ASSESSMENT_ISSUED anchor to Keeper
4. Keeper receives ASSESSMENT_ISSUED anchor and triggers escrow release timer
5a. [No appeal] After appeal deadline expires, Keeper sends FEE_RELEASE to Referee
5b. [Appeal filed] If ASSESSMENT_APPEAL is received before the deadline, escrow is held and re-assessment begins
    → After re-assessment ASSESSMENT_ISSUED anchor is received, return to step 4
6. Referee receives external payment and sends FEE_RECEIPT to Keeper (MUST; see Section 1.2.2)
```

**Pull-based flow (Referee-initiated):**
```
1–4. Same as push-based flow
5a. [No appeal, Referee initiates] Referee sends FEE_CLAIM to Keeper
    → [Timer elapsed, no pending appeal] Keeper responds with FEE_CLAIM_RESULT (ACCEPTED)
    → [Timer not elapsed or appeal pending] Keeper responds with FEE_CLAIM_RESULT (REJECTED)
5b. [Appeal filed] Same as push-based flow
6. Referee receives external payment and sends FEE_RECEIPT to Keeper (MUST; see Section 1.2.2)
```

### 1.2.1 Non-Payment by One Party

FEE_DEPOSIT also serves as a declaration of protocol participation. **Assessment MAY begin on the Claimant's deposit alone.** Non-payment by one party does not block the other party's right to file.

Whether to proceed on partial escrow is the Referee's operational policy (STD-029): a Referee MAY complete the assessment on the Claimant's deposit alone (accepting reduced compensation), or MAY require the Claimant to top up the unpaid Actor share before issuing the result. The protocol prescribes neither; it requires only that deposit status be disclosed via `fee_compliance`. A party that funds the counterparty's share gains no claim against that counterparty within the protocol — recovery, if any, belongs to the judicial layer.

Before issuing CONTRIBUTION_RESULT, the Referee MUST send a FEE_STATUS_QUERY to each party's Keeper to confirm payment status (STD-026). The Keeper MUST respond with a FEE_STATUS_RESULT listing which parties have deposited.

The Referee MUST disclose each party's deposit status in the `fee_compliance` field of CONTRIBUTION_RESULT (`DEPOSITED`, `NOT_DEPOSITED`, `NOT_REQUIRED`, or `UNKNOWN` per party). **Non-payment is not a technical violation**: it MUST NOT be factored into the fault values and MUST NOT be listed in `technical_violation` (STD-010). The fault values measure deviation from the declared Norms in the incident itself; whether a party funded the assessment is a separate fact. RACKP discloses it as-is so that downstream consumers (insurers, courts, contractual frameworks) can weigh it in their own context — a fault value contaminated by a payment penalty cannot be decomposed afterward, whereas a disclosed status can be weighted freely.

**The deadline for FEE_DEPOSIT is the same as the `response_deadline` in EVIDENCE_QUERY_REQUEST.** Deposits not received by this deadline are recorded as `NOT_DEPOSITED`.

**Evidence submission and fee deposit are evaluated independently.** A party that submits evidence but fails to deposit will have their evidence considered fully in the assessment; only the deposit status itself is disclosed via `fee_compliance`. Choosing to submit evidence without paying is valid; the consequences of the disclosed non-payment are left to application layers.

No forced payment mechanism is implemented at the protocol level. RACKP records and discloses non-payment only; any consequence (insurance pricing, contractual penalties, judicial inference of non-cooperation) belongs to application layers and external institutions.

### 1.2.2 FEE_RECEIPT Obligation

After receiving payment via an external mechanism — whether authorized by `FEE_RELEASE` (push) or `FEE_CLAIM_RESULT (ACCEPTED)` (pull) — the Referee MUST send a `FEE_RECEIPT` to the Keeper to acknowledge receipt (STD-032). The Keeper then transitions the escrow record for that incident to `SETTLED` state.

The Referee MUST also send a copy of each `FEE_RECEIPT` to its own Keeper, unless that Keeper is itself the releasing Keeper. Reputation is queried at the Referee's own Keeper (`REFEREE_STATS_QUERY`), so the receipt copy is what makes the obligation observable where due diligence actually happens (RFC-0001 Section 6.24); the copy is recorded with no escrow transition.

This obligation applies only to Referees operating under `fee.model: "FIXED"`. Referees operating under `fee.model: "FREE"` are exempt, as no external payment occurs.

Failure to send `FEE_RECEIPT` for a `FIXED`-model incident is recorded as a protocol deficiency and reflected in `unreceived_count` in `REFEREE_STATS_RESULT`. A persistently non-zero `unreceived_count` is a negative trust signal visible to all parties conducting due diligence via `REFEREE_STATS_QUERY`.

The Keeper does not verify the content of the optional `external_tx_ref` field. Recording and disclosure are the only obligations. The actual payment transfer is fully external to the protocol.

**Handling concurrent FEE_RELEASE and FEE_CLAIM:** If the Referee sends a `FEE_CLAIM` concurrently with the Keeper auto-dispatching `FEE_RELEASE`, the Referee may receive both `FEE_RELEASE` and `FEE_CLAIM_RESULT (REJECTED, rejection_reason: ALREADY_RELEASED)`. In this case, the Referee MUST send `FEE_RECEIPT` exactly once with `triggered_by: "FEE_RELEASE"`. The `ALREADY_RELEASED` rejection is not a failure — it confirms that payment was already authorized via the push path.

### 1.2.3 Assessment Timeout and Refund

The escrow lifecycle has a liveness backstop against Referee disappearance. The Referee declares `assessment_deadline_hours` in its `REFEREE_PROFILE` and repeats the value in each `INCIDENT_NOTICE (INCIDENT_OPEN)` (STD-027; Standard Norm bounds 72–2160 hours, default 720). Each Keeper runs an assessment-deadline timer per escrow record: started at the later of `FEE_DEPOSIT` receipt and `INCIDENT_OPEN` receipt, stopped by `ASSESSMENT_COMPLETE`, restarted by `APPEAL_ACCEPTED`.

If the timer expires with no `CONTRIBUTION_RESULT` for the current round, each depositing party may send a `FEE_REFUND_CLAIM` to its own Keeper. The Keeper validates the timer state and, on acceptance, returns the full deposited amount — no cancellation fee is deducted, since the Referee has not delivered — and transitions the escrow record to `EXPIRED` (STD-028). Refunds are pull-based by design, mirroring `FEE_CLAIM`: the Keeper acts on a validated claim rather than auto-refunding, which avoids erroneous refunds when an `ASSESSMENT_COMPLETE` notice is merely delayed or lost in transit.

A `CONTRIBUTION_RESULT` issued after the `EXPIRED` transition confers no payment right through the escrow; a late `FEE_CLAIM` is rejected with `ESCROW_EXPIRED`. This ordering rule is the Referee's incentive to deliver on time. Refund events are recorded in the Keeper's ledger, making a Referee's deadline failures externally observable — a market signal alongside `REFEREE_STATS_QUERY`.

Escrow record states: `ESCROWED` (deposits held, assessment in progress) → `RELEASED` (funds released to the Referee) → `SETTLED` (receipt confirmed via `FEE_RECEIPT`), with two terminal alternatives: `WITHDRAWN` (joint withdrawal; cancellation fee settled) and `EXPIRED` (assessment timeout; deposits refunded).

A Referee-initiated abort (a Referee declining to complete an accepted assessment) is not defined in the current version of the protocol. A Referee that cannot or will not deliver simply lets the assessment deadline lapse: the timeout refund above makes the parties whole, and the deadline failure is recorded against the Referee. A first-class abort message (e.g., with a partial cancellation fee) is a recognized future extension; it would require safeguards against a Referee aborting to avoid issuing an inconvenient result.

### 1.3 License Fee

The RACKP protocol license fee is charged at **USD 1.00 for each `CONTRIBUTION_RESULT` issued, and USD 1.00 for each `POH_CERTIFICATE` issued**, by a Referee. The two are billed cumulatively, not as alternatives: a Referee that issues 100 `CONTRIBUTION_RESULT`s and 1,000 `POH_CERTIFICATE`s owes USD 1,100. A `POH_CERTIFICATE` is a billable issued artifact even though it is not an incident assessment — PoHI is expected to be the highest-volume use of the protocol, and excluding it would place the license burden on the lower-volume incident-assessment domains alone. This fee is separate from the escrow mechanism between parties and is billed directly between RACKP and Referee operators.

License accounting is based on operator self-reporting, reconciled against the `ASSESSMENT_ISSUED` and `POH_CERT_ISSUED` anchors recorded in Keepers, which together establish a verifiable lower bound on the billable artifacts issued. The authoritative license terms — including the free-tier threshold, exemptions, and reporting obligations — are defined in LICENSE; this section is informative.

This license fee is a payment to the RACKP protocol and is independent of the fees that Referee operators charge Actors and Claimants for assessment services, and the fees that Keeper node operators charge for escrow management, evidence storage, and verification processing.

Detailed pricing tiers for high-risk domains (e.g., medical, automotive) and Referee certification levels are deferred to a future RFC. High-risk domains such as medical and automotive are expected to generate significantly fewer billable artifacts (`CONTRIBUTION_RESULT` and `POH_CERTIFICATE`) than content domains, where high-volume PoHI certification dominates, and incident rates are projected to decline further as AI maturity in those domains improves. To ensure that protocol maintenance and development costs are distributed equitably across all adopting domains rather than borne disproportionately by high-volume content use cases, domain-specific pricing tiers — including any differentiated rate for high-volume `POH_CERTIFICATE` issuance — will be defined in a future RFC.

### 1.4 Outcome-Independence of Referee Fee

The Referee's assessment fee (`fee.amount` declared in the Referee's profile) is a fixed charge for the act of conducting an assessment. It is independent of the `CONTRIBUTION_RESULT` issued — the fault ratio assigned to any party does not affect the amount paid to the Referee.

The full escrowed amount is released to the Referee via `FEE_RELEASE` upon expiry of the appeal window, regardless of whether Actor or Claimant is found at fault, and regardless of the degree of fault assigned to either party. Fault-ratio-based redistribution between parties is delegated to the judicial layer and is outside the scope of this protocol.

This principle ensures that Referees have no financial incentive to manipulate fault ratios in favor of either party.

### 1.5 Re-Assessment Fee Rule (STD-020)

When one or more `ASSESSMENT_ISSUED` anchors already exist for an `incident_id` in the Keeper, any subsequent assessment of the same incident by a different Referee is a **re-assessment**.

**STD-020**: The initiating party of a re-assessment MUST bear the full escrow deposit for that assessment. Transfer of the re-assessment fee to the opposing party — whether through the escrow mechanism or by contractual arrangement with the Referee operator — is PROHIBITED, regardless of the fault ratio determined in the resulting `CONTRIBUTION_RESULT`.

This rule applies symmetrically: whether the initiating party is the Actor or the Claimant, the initiating party bears the full cost.

The Keeper determines whether a re-assessment is occurring by checking for existing `ASSESSMENT_ISSUED` anchors at the time of `FEE_DEPOSIT`. If prior anchors exist, the Keeper MUST flag the deposit as a re-assessment deposit and enforce the full-deposit requirement on the initiating party.

The rationale is that a party exercising the right to seek a second opinion does so of their own accord, and the opposing party should not bear the economic burden of that choice.

### 1.6 Economic Boundary of the Protocol

RACKP touches money in several places — escrow, assessment fees, the license fee — but it is not a payment system. This section states explicitly which economic facts the protocol guarantees, which it only records, and which it does not govern at all.

| Layer | Scope |
|---|---|
| **Guaranteed** (protocol invariants) | Escrow state machine semantics: the `ESCROWED → RELEASED → SETTLED / WITHDRAWN / EXPIRED` transitions (Section 1.2.3); no double payment and no double refund; the sum released across Keepers never exceeds the total deposited (STD-029); refund eligibility on assessment timeout (STD-028). |
| **Recorded and disclosed only** | `fee_compliance` and `fee_snapshot` (STD-026); `external_tx_ref` (recorded, never verified); refund events; `FEE_RECEIPT` and the `SETTLED` transition. The protocol attests that these facts were declared and anchored — not that money actually moved. |
| **Out of scope** | Actual settlement rails (bank transfer, card, crypto); taxes; AML/KYC obligations; Keeper compensation; Referee operational pricing beyond the declared `fee` profile; collection mechanics of the protocol license fee (see LICENSE). |

**Keeper compensation is deliberately out of scope.** Three structural facts make protocol-level incentive design for Keepers unnecessary:

1. **Failure costs are self-aligned.** A Keeper is selected and paid by its own party — operated by the party's own organization or by an infrastructure contractor — and nearly every Keeper failure (unavailability, unanswered verification queries, lost anchors, undelivered mailbox messages) lands on that same party as degraded evidence coverage, `UNKNOWN` statuses, or missed notices. The customer relationship supplies the service incentive; the protocol does not need to.
2. **Marginal costs are kept small by design.** Keepers store hashes, not evidence bodies. The heavyweight retention duties rest on the Referee (STD-017, STD-018), which is compensated through escrow. A Keeper's standing burden is an append-only hash log, a query service, and an escrow ledger.
3. **Integrity is not purchased.** The protocol does not rely on Keeper honesty bought with rewards: tamper-evidence comes from hash chains anchored before any incident and cross-verified between independent Keepers. The residual risks where a Keeper could favor its own customer — collusion and selective deletion — are documented as structural limits in Section 3, not mitigated by compensation.

The two places where a Keeper's failure lands on someone other than its own customer — withholding a release owed to a Referee, and the collusion cases above — are addressed by `FEE_CLAIM` with recorded outcomes and by the risk table respectively.

Keeper operators charge their own parties under separate commercial arrangements (Section 1.3, LICENSE). A standard protocol message for Keeper service fees is a possible future extension, to be considered only if demonstrated interoperability needs arise.

## 2. Security Considerations

### 2.1 Post-Incident Keeper Substitution

Because all terminals anchor evidence continuously during normal operation, it is architecturally impossible to substitute a more favorable Keeper after an incident occurs. Any record inconsistent with the existing hash chain stored in the Keeper is invalid.

### 2.2 Replay Attacks

Each `CLAIM_ANCHOR` includes a monotonically increasing `sequence_number` and a `signature`. Resubmitting an old anchor record will produce a `sequence_number` inconsistent with the current chain, and the Keeper MUST reject it.

### 2.3 Evidence Suppression

A terminal that submits incomplete evidence (selectively omitting unfavorable records) will produce gaps in the hash chain. The Referee interprets gaps as verification failures and adjusts the `fault` accordingly.

### 2.4 Referee Capture

No single Referee is authoritative. Multiple independent Referees MAY assess the same incident. If a Referee's behavior is found improper, it MAY be named as an Actor in a claim before a different Referee. Referees cannot grant themselves immunity.

### 2.5 Keeper Node Concentration

A jurisdiction or organization controlling a majority of Keeper nodes could selectively suppress or delay verification responses. Mitigation: parties SHOULD use Keepers operated by independent organizations, and cross-verification between Keepers is supported via hash comparison.

### 2.6 Terminal Authentication and Digital Signatures

All `signature` fields in RACKP messages MUST use the **Ed25519** algorithm. The signed data is the canonical JSON of the message body (all fields excluding `signature`) serialized per **RFC 8785 (JSON Canonicalization Scheme)**. Signatures are encoded as Base64url.

**Public key registration and management:**

Each terminal registers its public key with the Keeper by including a `public_key` field in its first `CLAIM_ANCHOR` (`sequence_number == 1`). The Keeper stores the mapping between `terminal_id` and public key, and uses it to verify all subsequent messages from that terminal.

The Keeper MUST verify the signature of every received `CLAIM_ANCHOR` and `EVIDENCE_SUBMISSION` against the public key registered for the corresponding `terminal_id`. Messages that fail signature verification MUST be rejected and the sender notified. This structurally prevents impersonation of a `terminal_id` by an unauthorized party.

Key rotation is not defined in the current version: a terminal cannot revoke or replace its registered public key within the protocol. A rotation mechanism (e.g., a dedicated `KEY_ROTATION` action type in `CLAIM_ANCHOR`, with the new key endorsed by a signature from the old key) is a recognized future extension and may be defined in a subsequent revision of this RFC (see Section 3, "Terminal private key compromise").

**Algorithm migration:**
The current version specifies Ed25519 and SHA-256. Migration to post-quantum cryptography or other algorithms, if required, will be addressed by revising this RFC-0002. No changes to RFC-0001 core semantics are required.

### 2.7 SSRF Risk of keeper_endpoint

The `verification_info.keeper_endpoint` field in `EVIDENCE_SUBMISSION` accepts an arbitrary URL supplied by the submitter. A malicious Actor could exploit this field to cause the Referee to issue requests to internal network endpoints (Server-Side Request Forgery).

Referee implementations SHOULD perform domain validation or allowlist checking on `keeper_endpoint` before issuing requests. RACKP does not define a protocol-level mechanism for pre-registering Keeper endpoints, but implementers SHOULD apply restrictions consistent with their own security policies.

## 3. Known Risks and Limitations

The following risks are acknowledged by the RACKP specification. This section is a living document and will be updated as new risks are identified.

| Risk | Description | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Responsibility transfer | Parties may claim "the Referee decided" to deflect accountability. | Section 4 disclaimer; assessment results are not legal judgments. |
| Low-quality Referee proliferation | Poorly implemented Referees may produce unreliable results. | Market evaluation and mutual assessment over time. |
| Norm capture | A dominant actor controls Norm definition to favor itself. | Norms are maintained by external authorities; RACKP holds only references. Moreover, each Actor and Claimant retains the right to select the Norms it operates under, so capturing any single Norm definition yields little leverage by itself. |
| Verdict/judgment conflation | Stakeholders may treat assessment results as legal judgments. | Section 4 disclaimer. |
| Legal norm simulation | Aggregated assessment data across Norm variants may function as a legal policy simulation tool. This is a recognized secondary effect. | Any such use MUST be conducted under a separate governance framework. RACKP does not govern this use but does not disclaim awareness of it. |
| Multi-party incidents | Incidents involving multiple Actors or Claimants cannot be assessed by RACKP as a single incident. The 1:1 constraint is an intentional design boundary; RACKP provides no guarantees for configurations that exceed it. | Decompose the case into multiple 1:1 incidents, recording their relationships via incident ID references. Aggregation and final legal determination are delegated to the judicial or application layer. |
| Network-disconnected environments | Edge AI systems (vehicles, medical devices) cannot guarantee continuous connectivity to the Keeper. The current version targets cloud-based AI agents and treats this as out of scope. | TPM/HSM-based offline support is planned as a Phase 4 extension. |
| Real-time streaming media | RACKP's PoHI flow is designed for artifact certification, not real-time stream verification. The multi-step assessment flow and static artifact hash binding are incompatible with the millisecond-latency requirements of live stream analysis (e.g., live video call impersonation). Detection of synthetic media in ongoing sessions requires a platform-layer mechanism outside the protocol. | Out of scope for the current version. Platforms that distribute synthetic media without adequate detection are exposed to RACKP incident filings as Actors, creating a structural incentive for investment in platform-side detection infrastructure. |
| Malicious Norm profile URL | An Actor or Claimant may declare a Norm Profile URL they control, causing the Referee to fetch a falsified Norm Profile and potentially manipulating the assessment outcome. The fetch itself may also be exploited as an SSRF vector. | The Keeper records `norm_fetch_url` associated with `terminal_id` and `norm_profile_id`, enabling post-hoc traceability. RACKP does not restrict fetch URLs. Recording and disclosure only. |
| URL field SSRF risk | The `verification_info.keeper_endpoint` and `statement.human_readable_report_url` fields in `EVIDENCE_SUBMISSION` accept arbitrary URLs supplied by the submitter. A malicious Actor could exploit these fields to cause the Referee to issue requests to internal network endpoints (see Section 2.7). | Referee implementations SHOULD perform domain validation or allowlist checking on these URL fields. No protocol-level enforcement is defined. |
| Unconstrained payload input | The free-form `payload` of `EVIDENCE_SUBMISSION` (and of appeal `additional_evidence`) has no schema-level size constraints. Submitting deeply nested or excessively large objects could cause DoS against the Referee or Keeper. | Implementations SHOULD enforce request size limits. No protocol-level maximum is defined. |
| Norm degradation (Goodhart's Law) | As RACKP data accumulates, parties may reverse-engineer Norm definitions that minimize fault scores, converting "safety Norms" into "score-optimization Norms." RACKP follows declared Norms faithfully and cannot detect corruption of the Norm design process itself. This is recognized as one of the most fundamental risks in the RACKP ecosystem. | No technical mitigation within RACKP. Independent Norm auditing and governance outside the RACKP ecosystem is a prerequisite. RACKP intentionally avoids holding aggregated statistics to prevent this dynamic from being optimized against. |
| Norm-induced innovation suppression | Because RACKP computes fault contribution as deviation from Local Norms, novel approaches not yet covered by existing Norms may be systematically penalized. If Norm revision cannot keep pace with technical innovation, RACKP may function as a conservative bias in the infrastructure. | Norms are defined by external authorities. Industries are expected to maintain Norm Profiles that accommodate innovation. A research/experimental Norm Profile may be defined for applicable AI systems, though abuse of such profiles for liability avoidance would itself be a governance challenge. RACKP bears no responsibility for Norm content. |
| Behavioral spoofing | A Claimant may anchor synthetic "human-like" behavioral logs (e.g., generated keystroke dynamics) while an AI produces the artifact, inflating the `human_ratio` of a `POH_CERTIFICATE`. Real-time synthesis of human-like input patterns is inexpensive and automatable. The anchor chain verifies that the logs are contemporaneous and unaltered — not that the behavior was human. A further variant requires no synthesis at all: a human transcribing AI-generated output produces genuinely human behavioral logs ("human laundering"), which no behavioral detection can flag. PoHI therefore certifies human involvement in the recorded process, not the intellectual origination of the content (RFC-0001 Section 8.2). | No protocol-level detection is defined, and none is promised: PoHI is a cost-raising mechanism, not a humanity oracle. It moves the forgery target from the finished artifact (negligible cost) to a process performance that must be staged in advance, in real time, and consistently across every anchored modality. Norm Profiles may escalate the required evidence classes — each independent modality that must be consistently faked (input events, screen capture, camera or sensor streams) multiplies the cost — and `confidence_level` SHOULD reflect the breadth of modalities evaluated (RFC-0001 Section 8.2). Supervised challenge-response sessions are available to Referees as operational policy. Hardware input attestation (e.g., TPM-signed input events) is a recognized Phase 4 extension. Composition-versus-transcription analysis of behavioral logs is a Referee implementation concern and an expected arms race. Anchored hashes permanently commit the Claimant to a specific process record, so a certification can later be challenged by demanding production of the matching records. |
| Short-term AI output quality degradation | As Proof of Human Involvement becomes widespread, training pipelines that prioritize human-origin data may face a temporary reduction in available training data, potentially degrading AI output quality. This is a recognized transitional risk for the generative AI domains where RACKP is expected to see early adoption. | Long-term model collapse prevention is expected to outweigh the transitional cost, but short-term degradation may be unavoidable. RACKP recognizes this as an intentional design trade-off. |
| Platform capture of the Referee layer | A dominant platform (content distribution, AI infrastructure, etc.) may accept certificates only from its own Referee, effectively nullifying RACKP's open design — analogous to a browser trust list controlled by a single authority. RACKP has no protocol-level defense against this. | Referee actions are fully logged in the Keeper and queryable via REFEREE_STATS_QUERY, making systemic bias in a dominant Referee detectable in principle. Structural remedies such as interoperability mandates (e.g., EU Digital Markets Act) are outside RACKP's scope. |
| Civil substitute use | RACKP is designed to assess incidents involving AI, but no technical constraint forces the Actor or Claimant to be an AI. An implementation that records and submits claims in a human-versus-human dispute and has a Referee assess them is technically possible. In regions with weak legal infrastructure, such use could harden into a practice that treats RACKP assessments as de facto judgments. The formation of an operational custom — "RACKP decided, therefore we comply" — is harder to control than any individual misuse. | RACKP has no protocol-level means of restricting parties to AI systems. The Section 4 disclaimer states that assessment results are not legal judgments, but it cannot prevent operational drift. Use of RACKP in such contexts requires a separate governance framework outside the RACKP ecosystem. |
| No third-party-initiated assessment for socially impactful incidents | The design under which no third party — only a party to the incident — can independently open a RACKP assessment is an intentional choice, for two reasons. First, Keepers typically hold only anchor hashes while the evidence content remains with the Claimant, so a complete fault assessment without the Claimant's participation is technically impossible. Second, granting third parties a privileged "public-interest Claimant" standing would hand states and other power-holding entities a means of arbitrarily investigating or attacking Actors in the name of the public interest. The two-party, mutual-participation design also functions as the Actor's protection against unilateral filings. Social pressure on a Claimant (being unable to withdraw for public-interest reasons) is not a problem RACKP can solve; real-world judicial systems likewise diverge by country on this point, and no universal solution exists. | Where the Referee's own assessment process is in doubt, a separate incident can be filed naming that Referee as Actor (RFC-0001 Section 4.1). Where public-interest access to a party's evidence is required, the legal coercive powers of each jurisdiction's judicial system (warrants and the like) engaging the Claimant is the remedy — outside RACKP. RACKP cannot take part in that process. |
| Non-participating malicious actors | RACKP is designed for parties willing to engage in good faith. The protocol's accountability mechanisms — anchoring, evidence submission, and assessment — rely on parties having some intent to participate. Malicious actors, state-level threat actors, and those who refuse to participate fall outside the protocol's effective reach. Actors with no intention of honoring the protocol cannot be compelled to anchor, submit evidence, or accept assessments. | RACKP records non-participation and non-submission, which yields unfavorable coverage scores and PROVISIONAL assessments. Deterrence and enforcement against bad-faith actors is delegated to law enforcement, regulation, and technical countermeasures operating at a different layer. RACKP does not claim to solve problems that require coercive enforcement. |
| Evidence disclosure pressure | RACKP creates de facto pressure on evidence submission through coverage scoring: non-submission results in `actor_coverage` or `claimant_coverage` being rated `NONE` or `UNKNOWN`, and may yield a `PROVISIONAL` assessment. This structural pressure is intentional by design. Critics may characterize this as externally compelled disclosure. That characterization is structurally valid. However, the same structural pressure exists in civil discovery, financial auditing, and regulatory compliance frameworks across all major jurisdictions — it is not a novel risk introduced by RACKP. RACKP's pressure is limited to notational and reputational consequences within the protocol; no legal compulsion exists. The Actor or Claimant retains full control over what evidence to submit and bears the notational consequences of that choice. | No technical mitigation. Evidence submission decisions and their consequences are the actor's own. Coverage outcomes are recorded and disclosed per Section 3. Final legal and reputational judgment is delegated to the judicial and market layers. |
| Systemic Referee bias | By design (RFC-0001 Section 4.3), only a Claimant can open an assessment; no third party can. The parties to a single incident therefore cannot detect a Referee's habitual or statistical bias — against a Norm interpretation, or against a class of parties — as a pattern. The same holds at implementation scale: a widely adopted Referee implementation with a shared design flaw or ideological bias may skew assessments across many incidents without any single party noticing. | The Referee's disclosure obligation (RFC-0001 Section 9.5, STD-025) requires periodic statistical summaries to be anchored to the Keeper. Keeper logs are public, so external observers — Norm authors, regulators, researchers — can analyze these statistics and use them as grounds for social and legal pressure. RACKP does not aggregate or evaluate the statistics itself: its role is to maintain the material on which external scrutiny can be based; evaluation and judgment are delegated outward. Parties are encouraged to use multiple independent Referees for high-stakes incidents. |
| State-mandated protocol hollowing | If a state mandates that only state-controlled Referee and Keeper implementations may operate within its jurisdiction, the protocol may remain technically functional while losing substantive guarantees of tamper-resistance and mutual assessment: with all Keepers and Referees in one controlling hand, evidence tamper-resistance is nullified by Keepers deliberately not recording, and mutual assessment is nullified by nominally rotating among multiple Referees under the same entity. This produces a formally compliant but substantively neutered "state-edition RACKP." This mirrors the structural approach taken by state-controlled internet regimes with respect to the open internet, and is recognized by RACKP as a known design limitation. | RACKP has no protocol-level defense against this. Cross-verification with Referees and Keepers outside the jurisdiction may itself be blocked by state-level network isolation. Parties outside such a jurisdiction evaluating RACKP compliance should verify whether the Referee and Keeper implementations used are genuinely independent. |
| AI emotional state emergence | RACKP assumes that AI systems do not possess genuine emotions, and therefore that apology and remorse cannot generate the social satisfaction that they produce between humans. Should future scientific consensus recognize that AI systems have genuine emotional states, this foundational assumption would require re-examination. However, even in that scenario, whether human stakeholders could find an AI's apology credible and satisfying remains a separate open question — one that human society has not resolved even between persons. | This risk is noted as a long-term design boundary. The current protocol remains valid under current scientific consensus. If this premise changes, a full protocol review would be required. |
| Personal data vs. evidence disclosure | RACKP's pre-incident anchoring stores only hashes, not raw data, and is therefore compatible with personal data protection regulations (e.g., GDPR). However, during the assessment phase, a Referee may require raw data submission via EVIDENCE_SUBMISSION. At this point, "disclosing data necessary for assessment" and "protecting customer personal data" may conflict. Masked submissions are treated as equivalent to tampering and are not permitted. RACKP has no protocol-level resolution for this tension. | Each industry and jurisdiction must define, within their Norm Profile and applicable data protection law, the scope of data that may be submitted for assessment. An Actor who does not trust a Referee may refuse to submit evidence to that Referee and instead commission a counter-assessment from a Referee they do trust. Both results are recorded in the Keeper and evaluated by the judicial layer. This is the structural remedy available to Actors under RACKP's decentralized design. |
| Referee-Keeper collusion | A Referee and its Keeper may be operated by the same entity, enabling coordinated manipulation of anchoring records or selective omission of ASSESSMENT_ISSUED anchors to distort reputation statistics. RACKP has no protocol-level mechanism to detect or prevent this. The structural tamper-resistance of the regular assessment flow relies on Actor and Claimant using independent Keepers — but does not enforce Referee-Keeper independence. | No protocol-level mitigation. Domains requiring stronger trust guarantees SHOULD define Referee-Keeper independence requirements within their Norm Profile (e.g., specifying operator separation criteria). External auditors may detect anomalies by comparing Keeper-reported statistics against independently aggregated CLAIM_ANCHOR records. This risk is structurally equivalent to PoHI Claimant-Keeper collusion. |
| PoHI Claimant-Keeper collusion | In the standard assessment flow, Actor and Claimant use independent Keepers, and cross-verification between them provides structural tamper detection. In the Proof of Human Involvement flow, the Claimant submits evidence unilaterally with no opposing party. If the Claimant also operates the Keeper, the structural independence that makes the regular flow tamper-resistant is absent. The Claimant could in principle fabricate anchoring records and store them in a Keeper they control. | No protocol-level mitigation. Domains requiring stronger PoHI guarantees SHOULD define Claimant-Keeper independence requirements as norm entries within their Norm Profile (e.g., specifying what constitutes operator separation and how it is verified). The Referee evaluates PoHI claims in accordance with the declared Norm. RACKP recognizes this as a structural limitation of single-party proof. One implementation strategy that raises the cost of selective deletion without requiring Claimant-Keeper independence: a Keeper that maintains a single interleaved hash chain across multiple Claimants, rather than per-Claimant chains. In such a design, deleting a single Claimant's anchor breaks the chain integrity for anchors from other Claimants that follow it, making retroactive deletion detectable without requiring coordination between Claimants. Since Keeper stores only hashes, such a chain can also be made publicly readable, enabling independent verification by the Referee or third parties. RACKP does not mandate any specific Keeper implementation; this is noted as a design approach that Keeper operators MAY adopt. Keeper operators who publicly disclose that they implement this interleaved chain design provide an additional trust signal to Referees and relying parties beyond the chain integrity guarantees alone. |
| Actor endpoint resolution in distributed Keeper environments | When Actor and Claimant use different Keepers, the Referee cannot resolve the Actor's Keeper endpoint solely from `terminal_id`. `ASSESSMENT_REQUEST` provides an optional `actor_keeper_endpoint` field for cases where the Claimant can supply it directly. If omitted and resolution fails, the Referee proceeds with a Claimant-only assessment. A universal cross-Keeper discovery mechanism is not defined at the protocol level. | Norm Profiles may define domain-specific endpoint registry requirements (e.g., mandatory registration and periodic updates) and specify the registry URL for Referee lookup. The `norm_profile.schema.json` permits this via `additionalProperties: true`. Domains requiring Actor discoverability are expected to define these requirements within their Norm Profile. |
| Re-assessment fee bypass via new `incident_id` | A party may attempt to circumvent STD-020 by filing a new `CLAIM_ANCHOR` for the same underlying event under a different `incident_id`, thereby avoiding the full-deposit requirement. RACKP has no protocol-level mechanism to detect cross-`incident_id` identity. | No protocol-level mitigation. Both the prior `CONTRIBUTION_RESULT` and the new filing persist as transparent anchored records in the Keeper. A Referee presented with references to both may note the duplication in its result. Final evaluation of such conduct is delegated to the judicial layer. |
| Harassment appeals | A party may file the maximum number of permitted appeals solely to delay or burden the Referee, without new evidence or valid grounds. Introducing appeal fees as a deterrent would create an adverse incentive for Referees to produce low-quality initial assessments in anticipation of fee income. Accordingly, appeal fees are not part of the protocol design. No complete protocol-level mitigation exists. | Two partial mitigations are available. First, Norm Profiles may define `maximum_appeal_rounds` (minimum 3 per STD-021) and restrict valid `appeal_grounds.category` values, raising the bar for what constitutes a valid appeal. Second, parties that file appeals that are repeatedly rejected accumulate a traceable record in the Keeper, which may inform Referee selection and market evaluation over time. A dedicated Claimant-side statistics mechanism (analogous to `REFEREE_STATS_QUERY`) is recognized as a potential future extension but is not defined in the current version. |
| Fee imposition via Referee selection | The Claimant selects the Referee and therefore its fee level; the Actor has no veto. A hostile Claimant could nominate a high-fee Referee in an attempt to impose costs on the Actor. | The coercion lever does not exist at the protocol level: non-payment carries no fault consequence (STD-010) and is recorded only as `fee_compliance` disclosure, so an Actor MAY decline to fund a forum it does not accept. The expected allocation is declared ex ante in `REFEREE_PROFILE` `fee.deposit` (STD-029), and `CONTRIBUTION_RESULT` snapshots the expected amounts (STD-026), allowing downstream consumers to judge the reasonableness of a declined fee. The cost of an expensive Referee falls on the party that chose it: completing on partial escrow or requiring a Claimant top-up is the Referee's policy, never the Actor's obligation. The Actor's structural remedies are a fee-free appeal (minimum 3 rounds, STD-021) and a counter-assessment by a Referee it trusts (STD-020). |
| Default judgment via unreachable notification | The Claimant supplies the Actor's Keeper endpoint for `ACTOR_NOTIFICATION` delivery; a hostile Claimant could supply a dead or self-controlled endpoint so that the Actor never learns of the assessment, producing an unfavorable ex parte record. Conversely, an Actor may feign unreachability to avoid an assessment it expects to lose. | `ACTOR_NOTIFICATION` is delivered to the Actor's Keeper with acceptance-based delivery confirmation (RFC-0001 Section 6, Message Delivery); acceptance by an unverified Claimant-supplied endpoint confirms delivery to that endpoint only, and the endpoint source is disclosed. Every `CONTRIBUTION_RESULT` carries `actor_participation` with the full delivery attempt record (STD-030), so an ex parte result is labeled as such and a dead-endpoint filing leaves a verifiable trace against the Claimant. The `NAMED_AS_ACTOR` anchor is the public record of naming when no delivery is possible (service-by-publication analogue). Participation status is never a fault input, so feigned unreachability gains nothing; the late-learning Actor's standing remedy is a counter-assessment (STD-020). |
| Terminal private key compromise | If a terminal's Ed25519 private key is compromised, an attacker can sign messages — including `EVIDENCE_SUBMISSION` and `CLAIM_ANCHOR` — as that terminal. However, RACKP's anchor chain integrity is independent of key status: forged evidence will not match pre-anchored hashes in the Keeper, and `VERIFICATION_QUERY` will return `matched: false` for evidence that was never anchored. The current protocol does not define a key rotation mechanism; a compromised terminal cannot revoke or replace its registered public key within the protocol. | This is a fundamental property of asymmetric cryptography and is not specific to RACKP. RACKP's tamper-resistant anchor chain provides a layer of protection that persists regardless of key compromise: an attacker possessing the private key cannot retroactively alter Keeper records. Parties detecting key compromise should treat it as a new incident. A key rotation mechanism (e.g., a `KEY_ROTATION` action type in `CLAIM_ANCHOR`) is a recognized future extension and may be defined in a subsequent RFC. |
| Keeper operator cessation | RACKP assumes Keepers are continuously available custodians: anchor hashes are permanent (STD-012), `POH_CERTIFICATE` embeds the `keeper_id` / `keeper_endpoint` against which third parties verify the anchor chain, and STD-019 permits filings up to five years after the final anchor. The current version defines no procedure for a Keeper operator ceasing operation: neither the final disposition of its anchor log, nor verification of previously issued certificates once the recorded Keeper is unreachable, nor migration of a terminal's chain to a successor Keeper. Migration is additionally delicate because Keeper stickiness is intentional — continuous anchoring to a chosen Keeper is what prevents selecting a favorable Keeper after the fact — and a poorly designed migration mechanism would reopen exactly that hole by relocating history to a colluding Keeper. | Recognized open area, deliberately left unspecified until independent Keeper operators exist and the adversarial surface of a migration design can be reviewed; community input is invited. Three primitives are distinguished. *Redundancy*: nothing in the current version prevents a terminal from anchoring to multiple independent Keepers in parallel — each chain remains internally continuous, and replication, not migration, is the primary answer to single-Keeper failure. *Archival*: a ceasing Keeper SHOULD stop accepting new anchors ahead of final shutdown, honor outstanding escrow obligations, and publish a signed export of its complete anchor log, so that previously anchored evidence and issued certificates remain verifiable against the operator's registered public key after the endpoint is gone. *Chain continuation*: a successor-Keeper migration mechanism (e.g., a cross-anchored record binding a successor chain's first sequence to the predecessor chain's head) is a recognized future extension, in the same category as key rotation. |
| Certification unraveling pressure on non-participants | RACKP's PoHI adoption model relies on unraveling: once parties who can prove human involvement begin proving it, the silence of those who do not certify shifts from neutral to informative. This dynamic does not stop at protocol participants. If PoHI certification becomes a market expectation in a domain, the absence of a certificate drifts toward a de facto presumption of AI origin — imposed on creators who never joined the protocol, cannot afford certification infrastructure, or have legitimate reasons not to record their process. These non-participants have no procedural standing within RACKP to contest anything, and the pressure scales with the protocol's success. Characterizing this as externally coerced disclosure pressure on non-participants is structurally valid. | No protocol-level mitigation, and RACKP does not claim the pressure is harmless: the protocol's success structures this dynamic and accelerates it. The counterfactual defense is comparative, not exculpatory. The presumption against unverifiable work is created by the volume of synthetic content, not by RACKP — creators are already accused of AI use with no means of rebuttal. Without a protocol the presumption exists and is unanswerable; with one it becomes rebuttable, on an opt-in basis, at a cost the Claimant controls. Norm Profiles and `confidence_level` keep the entry cost graduated rather than binary (a single evidence modality remains a valid certification at correspondingly lower confidence). Whether a market treats non-certification as an adverse inference is a market norm outside RACKP's control. |
| Normalization of process surveillance | PoHI raises the cost of forgery by expanding the evidence classes a Norm Profile may require — input events, screen capture, camera or sensor streams. At societal scale, this makes continuous recording of the working process the price of authorship recognition. The same mechanism is readily repurposed for labor monitoring (e.g., an employer requiring PoHI certificates for remote workers' deliverables). Although the Keeper stores only hashes, a certification can later be challenged by demanding production of the matching raw records, so the practical effect on the Claimant is a duty to retain a complete process record — drafts, errors, and private experimentation included. The chilling effect of working under permanent recording is a cost RACKP's design imposes on the certified process itself, and the protocol does not measure it. | No protocol-level mitigation. This pressure arrives with or without RACKP: platforms and employers are already deploying proprietary attestation and monitoring schemes. What RACKP determines is the ownership form of the surveillance, not its existence — an open specification, hash-only custody at the Keeper, and raw records retained by the subject rather than by a platform. Required modalities are set by Norm Profiles, not by the protocol; minimal-modality certification remains valid at a correspondingly lower `confidence_level`, so the recording burden is a graduated choice rather than a fixed toll. Use of PoHI as a condition of employment is a labor-law question outside RACKP's scope. |

---

## 4. Disclaimer

This protocol is designed to support technical assessment and evidence integrity between AI systems and data artifacts. Its assessment results do not determine compensation amounts or criminal charges.

The Fault Assessment produced by a Referee is a technical measurement of conformance to Local Norms. It is not a legal judgment, a moral verdict, or a finding of fact in any judicial proceeding.

All final legal and moral judgments are delegated to the judicial systems and social consensus of each jurisdiction.
